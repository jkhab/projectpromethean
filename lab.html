The Lab

Built, shipped, and running.

For ten years I ran support for enterprise software companies and kept a list of things I wished I could build. I never learned to code, so the list sat there. Then AI took the excuse away. This past year I built more than in the ten years before it, and the projects on this page are what happened when the wall between knowing what to build and being able to build it came down.

Customer Support

The Institutional-Knowledge Assistant

Claude · enterprise internal deployment

An internal AI assistant I built at my enterprise B2B SaaS day job. I loaded in a decade of product knowledge and the judgment patterns that usually take years to absorb, so instead of answering the same questions over and over, I answered each one once and the assistant generates the response from then on. As the person who onboards every new hire, it became an evergreen, on-demand source of that institutional knowledge: a new agent can get the senior answer at any hour without waiting for me.

Built and run inside a two-person team that absorbed 44% year-over-year ticket growth at a 72% single-reply resolution rate. It normalized response patterns, compressed new-hire ramp time, and reduced internal escalations, and it follows the rule I write about: ground every answer in real documentation, measure the customer's outcome instead of the deflection, and keep a person between the draft and the send.

Voice AI

OfferLetter

Claude · browser voice capture · Cloudflare Pages · retired

A web form covering every field of the California purchase agreement, driven by voice. The agent speaks the deal in plain language, the fields populate across the form, the address geocodes itself, and the finished data maps into the official CAR PDF, routed to the buyer for review and then to the listing agent. Thirty to forty-five minutes of form entry became about five minutes of talking.

Built for how agents actually work: profile auto-fill, support for up to three buyers, contingency math, close-of-escrow as days or a date, and voice input that never makes you narrate field names.

Status: built, demonstrated, and deliberately retired. Its browser-side API key got drained, and that bill became the guardrail list in Receipts below. Stack: Cloudflare Pages, browser voice capture, Claude API via Make.com, Google Maps geocoding.

In Progress

Recovery Navigator

Static web app · local storage only · no accounts by design · in progress

A step-by-step recovery roadmap built with a close friend who lost his home in the Eaton fire: the deadlines, agencies, and paperwork that arrive after the news trucks leave, sequenced so the right thing surfaces at the right time. Contacts live in a shared resources directory, so every agency gets verified once instead of once per step it appears on.

The defining decision: no accounts and no server. Fire survivors are prime scam targets, so recovery data never leaves their device (local storage plus a manual export file). The intake never assumes, either: it asks where you are in the process and only marks done what you confirm you've actually handled.

Status: in progress. Every step gets verified with the survivor it was built for before it goes live.

Receipts

The two questions every AI system gets asked.

Money and safety. Most pitches answer both with adjectives, so here are the actual numbers and the actual architecture from the app above, plus one lesson I paid for.

Unit Economics

What a $5.99 subscriber actually costs

A subscription with a recurring AI cost only works if the math is done before launch. Mine was. The pieces:

  • Net revenue: $5.99 a month, less Apple's commission at the small-developer rate, lands around $5.09.
  • The dominant cost is long conversations. Prompt caching on every turn re-reads the history at a fraction of full price and cuts a long session's input cost roughly in half.
  • Fair-use caps: a soft cap at 40 AI turns a day, where the guide starts pointing toward a meeting or a phone call, and a hard cap at 80 with a warm pause until tomorrow. Crisis messages are exempt from both.
  • A hard monthly spend ceiling at the API account, sized to the user base and raised deliberately, never automatically.
  • The result: even a heavy daily voice user pencils out to roughly $3 a month of margin, modeled before the App Store ever saw the build.
Safety Architecture

The one path that bypasses everything

In a recovery app, the most important architecture decision is the exception. Crisis language is checked on every single turn, and when the check fires, every commercial gate in the product stands down.

Message arrives
→
Crisis check, every turn
→
Caps, trial, paywall yield
→
Crisis resources, human handoff

The daily cap, the trial limit, and the paywall all step aside. The guide also stops being clever: its only job becomes pointing at people, the 988 line, and a meeting. Crisis resources are phone numbers, never a feature, and never gated. Even the fair-use pause message leads with the crisis line.

Tuition

One expensive lesson

OfferLetter, the voice tool above, shipped with an API key reachable from the browser. Someone found it and drained it, and I shut the deployment down. Everything I run now assumes that person exists:

  • Keys live server-side, behind a relay. The client never holds one.
  • Per-IP rate limits on the relay: 20 requests a minute, 300 a day.
  • A model whitelist, so a borrowed endpoint can't be pointed at a bigger, costlier model.
  • Hard monthly caps with alerts before the ceiling, and a kill switch that can turn a feature off over the air without an app release.

Every line on that list exists because of one bill.
