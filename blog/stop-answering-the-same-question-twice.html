Stop Answering the Same Question Twice

Jun 1, 2026 · 4 min read

Everyone pitching support AI wants to put a bot on your website. The best place to start is internal: getting what your most experienced people know out of their heads.

The most useful AI I ever built for a support team was not for customers. It was for me.

For years I held the knowledge at an enterprise software company. The product's edges, the workarounds, the right answer to the questions that came up over and over. I also onboarded every new hire. That made me useful. It also made me a bottleneck. Everything ran through one head, and the day that head leaves, the knowledge goes with it.

So I built a tool on the Claude API to fix that. Loading the docs was the easy part. The real work was writing down the judgment that only lived in my head, one answer at a time: the edge cases, the workarounds, the reason a setting looks wrong until you know the story behind it. Instead of answering the same one for the fortieth time, I answered it once into the tool, and it drafted from then on.

One thing I want to be clear about, because it is where these projects go sideways. The everyday repeat questions, the real FAQ, should be plain macros. A canned answer is safer and cheaper when you already know the answer. The AI earns its place on the messy long-tail questions: the new ones, and the ones that pull from five different docs no macro covers.

What it changed

The work did not vanish, it changed shape. Answering the same question for the fortieth time turned into writing it down once and keeping it current, which is a fraction of the load. And keeping it current matters. Captured answers go stale. The product ships a release, a workaround gets fixed, and an answer that was right last quarter is now wrong. Someone has to own keeping it true. Going internal first makes that visible: when an agent flags a draft as off, you just found a stale answer.

I did not expect the next part. It became the onboarding tool. The new hire at 2am who would normally sit on a question until morning gets the senior answer right then, without waiting for me. The knowledge stopped depending on whether I was at my desk.

Why start internal

Every support leader getting pitched AI is getting pitched the customer-facing version: a bot on the website. That is the wrong place to begin. Start internal, for three reasons.

A wrong answer gets caught in-house. An internal tool drafts for an agent who reads it before it goes anywhere. The error gets caught by your own people instead of by a customer. That is the cheap place to find mistakes.

Your own people tell you fast when it is off. Agents flag a bad answer immediately, in a way customers never will. Every flag is a stale answer found for free, a running signal instead of a once-a-year audit.

It captures the knowledge anyway. A customer-facing bot needs your knowledge written down and structured, and most teams find out halfway through a vendor rollout that it never was. It lived in a few heads. Building the internal tool is how you finally get it down. Capture finally pays off for the person holding it. He stops getting pinged.

The one rule

Ground it in your docs, show which doc each answer came from, and keep a person between the draft and the send. Build it so the tool says it does not know instead of guessing when it has nothing. The new hire at 2am is the highest-value user and the one least able to catch a wrong answer, so the tool has to show its source and route the hard ones up.

Done right, your most experienced person stops being a bottleneck and becomes a multiplier. The knowledge is there for everyone, all the time, and it does not leave when they do.

The cheap win nobody demos

The flashy projects get the demos and the budget. The quiet one, getting the knowledge out of your best people's heads, is cheaper, safer, and pays off faster. A two-person team I ran absorbed 44% year-over-year ticket growth at a 72% single-reply resolution rate by systematizing knowledge: macros, automated workflows, a knowledge base. The internal tool is that same move, pointed at the questions the macros never covered.

Cheaper is not free. You pay a real bill to run it, and someone has to keep the answers current. That does not kill the case. It just means you cost it out like an operation, not a demo.

Build that first. The harder question is whether it is actually working. I wrote about how to tell here.
