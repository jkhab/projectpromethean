I Built a Support Agent That Reads Your Help Center and Learns

Jun 14, 2026 · 8 min read

You load your help center into it once. It answers customer questions from those docs, with citations, and it refuses to guess when the docs come up short. Every time a person approves an answer, it remembers, so the same question is never hard twice. I built it, it runs on a laptop, and below it is answering real questions on a real help center you can go check yourself.

For most of ten years in support, I was a lookup table with a pulse. The same dozen questions, over and over, plus the handful that only I knew the answer to because the answer lived in my head and nowhere else. New hires waited on me. Customers waited on the queue. And the most valuable thing in the building, what the senior people actually knew, was the least accessible thing in the building.

The fix I eventually built at work was not a customer-facing chatbot. It was an assistant for the team: load the knowledge in once, and stop being the bottleneck. It quietly let a tiny team absorb a lot of growth. I cannot show you that one, so I rebuilt the idea from scratch, in the open. This is what it does, shown working, and how it helps.

One thing first: this is not advice on which support AI to buy. I build the thing and show you the parts that do not demo well. That is the only kind of authority worth having here.

What it does, in one breath

Load your help center once. Ask it a customer's question. It drafts an answer using only your own articles, shows you which ones it used, and hands it to a person to review and send. If your docs do not cover the question, it says so and routes to a human instead of inventing something. And when a person approves an answer, it saves it, so next time it already knows.

Watch it work, on a real help center

To make this checkable, I pointed it at Medium's public help center, about two hundred articles, and asked it real questions. The screens below are its real answers, shown the way they appear in the tool.

To earn money from your writing, join the Medium Partner Program, which lets you earn based on member engagement with your paywalled stories. The process: check eligibility (a complete profile, at least 6 published stories, 3 months of activity, a bank account in a supported country, and you must be 18+), apply at medium.com/partner-program, connect a Stripe account, and place your stories behind the paywall. Sources used: Medium Partner Program overview · Partner Program payouts · Make a story eligible to earn · Application and enrollment

That is correct, specific, and every fact in it traces to a named article a reviewer can open. It did not pull the $10 minimum or the Stripe detail out of thin air; it pulled them out of Medium's own docs.

Support Answer Assistant
Question: Can I connect Medium to my company's Salesforce CRM?
Not covered — needs a human
I'm sorry, but the help center and approved answers don't cover connecting Medium to Salesforce or any CRM, so I can't advise on that. I'd recommend having a human teammate follow up on this one.
No sources to stand on, so it gets out of the way instead of bluffing.

This is the part most demos skip, and it is the whole game. An ungrounded bot answers this confidently and wrongly. This one looked, found nothing to stand on, and got out of the way. A tool that does this is one your team trusts. A tool that bluffs is one they quietly stop using after it burns them once.

The part that makes it matter: it learns

That Salesforce question is a gap. So a human answers it once, and approves that answer. Watch what happens the next time someone asks the same thing:

Support Answer Assistant
Question: Can I connect Medium to my company's Salesforce CRM? (asked again, later)
Answered from a learned answer
Medium does not offer a built-in Salesforce or CRM integration. If you need Medium data in a CRM, you can export your stats from the Stats page and import them, or use a third-party automation tool to move the data over.

It went from "I don't know, escalate" to a clean, instant answer, because a person taught it once. That is the engine. Every escalation today becomes self-service tomorrow. The knowledge that used to live in one senior head gets captured the first time it is needed and is on tap from then on. This is the stop answering the same question twice idea, made real.

How it actually works

Three moving parts, and the clever part is which one does the work.

It loads your whole help center once and keeps it. You point it at your help center, it reads every published article in one pass and holds onto them. After that you just ask. There is no re-importing, no spoon-feeding it articles per question.

The real work is the instructions, not the model. When a question comes in, the model gets your articles, your team's approved answers, and a short set of rules. Those rules are the product:

Answer using ONLY the approved answers and help-center articles
provided. Do not use outside knowledge.

If a team-approved answer covers it, prefer it.
Otherwise cite the help-center article(s) you used.

If neither covers the question, say so plainly and recommend a
human take it. Do not guess, and do not stitch together a
plausible answer the sources don't support.

Never invent steps, prices, URLs, or policies.

"Use only the sources" is what stops it answering from the model's general memory, which sounds fine and is sometimes quietly wrong about your product. "Cite what you used" means a human verifies in seconds. And making "I don't know" a correct answer is what earns the whole thing its trust.

A person stays between the draft and the customer. The output is a draft. Someone reads it, edits if needed, and sends. Resolution is not the bot replying, it is the customer getting what they came for, and a human in the loop protects that while the system earns its keep. I went deeper on measuring that gap here.

How it helps, concretely

The new hire on a Tuesday night gets the senior answer without waiting for the senior person. The expert answers each new thing once instead of fifty times. The questions nobody wrote down get written down the moment they are asked. And because every answer shows its sources and passes a human, trust builds instead of eroding. On a small team that is the difference between drowning as volume grows and absorbing it.

Using it day to day

It runs as an app on your own computer. Load your help center once. Type a question, read the draft, check the sources, edit if needed, send. Click approve to teach it. That is the loop. Your knowledge and your API key stay on your machine; nothing is uploaded.

The honest limits

It is only as good as the docs behind it. A confident answer built on a stale article is worse than no answer, which is why step zero is auditing the help center, not adding the AI. I wrote about that here, and the tool I use to find the rot is here. It also has no memory of the specific customer, and the per-answer cost is small but real, so in front of a team you would put a hard spending cap and a kill switch on it, lessons I paid for elsewhere.

The point

The whole thing is about a hundred and fifty lines and runs on a laptop with no setup beyond a key. The leverage was never the model. It is the judgment about what to ground it on, what to let it say, where to keep a person, and how to make it learn from the people who already know the answers. That judgment is the thing I actually have, and this is what it looks like built.
