Fix the Operation Before You Add the AI

Jun 5, 2026 · 3 min read

AI scales the parts of your operation you have written down. It skips the judgment that lives in your best people's heads. On a clean operation that is a win. On a messy one the wrong answers pile up exactly where your docs are thinnest.

Most teams reach for AI to skip the boring work of fixing their operation. It does not fix a broken process. It runs the broken process with more confidence than your people ever did. It only scales what you have written down: the macros, the KB answers, the routing rules. The judgment that rescues a hard ticket lives in your agents' heads, and it cannot touch that.

Where it goes wrong

Point a good model at clean categories and current docs, and it makes a good operation faster. Point it at stale docs and tickets nobody agrees on, and it produces wrong answers at machine speed, to more customers than a person could. And the mess does not spread evenly. It piles up where the AI is most confident, which is where your docs are thinnest: your newest features and edge cases, your highest-stakes tickets.

This is why teams get blindsided when AI makes things worse. A broken operation looks healthier than it is, because people quietly patch it all day. They route by gut around a bad category system. They fill doc gaps from memory. That patch lives in the people, not the system. AI removes it and the cracks show all at once. It did not add them, it stopped hiding them.

Fix three things first

  • What a ticket is about and where it goes. If a person cannot say it reliably, the AI cannot either. It will guess wrong the same way every time.
  • The knowledge itself. Stale docs are worse than missing docs. With nothing there, the AI can stay quiet. With the wrong old version there, it answers confidently and wrong. So write it down, including the judgment your senior agents use on edge cases. That is the layer AI skips.
  • What resolved even means. When that is fuzzy, your deflection rate climbs as customers give up and stop replying, and you report it as a win. The number improves as the operation gets worse. Pin it down before AI gets near it. (More on that here.)

The order that works

Systematize first, then automate, then add AI. Clean the process by hand: macros, escalation procedures, a knowledge base that resolves repeat issues without a second contact. Then automate the mechanical parts: routing, acknowledgments, data entry. Add AI last, on top of what already works.

Done in that order, a small team absorbs a lot. A two-person team I ran took on 44% ticket growth year over year at a 72% single-reply resolution rate. The by-hand systematization carried that number. The AI was the last piece, and not for the bulk. It was for the long tail, the questions that do not deserve a macro. Add it because you know the exact problem it solves, not to lift a number the macros lifted already.

And decide how it fails safe before you turn it on: hard caps, a kill switch, a human fallback when it is unsure. Keep a person between the draft and the send.

A test you can run Monday

You do not need a vendor to find out if you are ready. Pull fifty resolved tickets. Have two people, on their own, categorize them and mark each one resolved or not. Then see how often they disagree. If they cannot agree on what a ticket is about and whether it was solved, the AI has nothing stable to learn from.

Most teams skip the cleanup because it does not demo the way AI does. Use that. The pressure to add AI is the best lever you will get to fund it. The teams that win did the boring part first. The ones that get burned bought the multiplier before they had something worth multiplying.
