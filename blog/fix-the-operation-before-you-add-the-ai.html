Fix the Operation Before You Add the AI

Jun 13, 2026 · 4 min read

AI amplifies whatever it is dropped onto. Put it on a clean support operation and it scales the good. Put it on a messy one and it scales the mess, faster and more confidently.

Most teams treat AI as a way to skip the boring work of fixing their support operation. It is the opposite. AI does not repair a broken process. It runs the broken process faster, at scale, and with more confidence than the humans ever did.

I learned this the slow way. Over a decade running support inside enterprise software, I rebuilt the same function more than once: an Intercom-to-Zendesk migration, then a move onto Salesforce, each one architected from scratch. Automation rules, routing logic, escalation paths, a knowledge base. The systems that made AI useful later were the ones I had already gotten right by hand.

What amplify actually means

A multiplier works in both directions. Point a good model at a support operation with clean categories, current documentation, and clear escalation paths, and it makes a good operation faster. Point the same model at one where tickets are miscategorized, the knowledge base is two years stale, and nobody agrees on what resolved means, and it produces wrong answers at machine speed, confidently, to more customers than a person ever could. The mess does not get hidden. It gets distributed.

What to fix first

Before any AI, three things have to be real.

  • The taxonomy and routing. If a person cannot reliably say what a ticket is about and where it should go, the model cannot either. It will guess, and it will guess wrong consistently.
  • The knowledge. AI grounded in stale or missing documentation inherits every gap. Writing down what currently lives in people's heads is the prerequisite, not an afterthought.
  • The definition of done. If the team cannot agree on what a resolved ticket actually means, no AI metric built on top of it will mean anything either.

The order that works

The sequence that worked for me was always the same: systematize, then automate, then add AI. First make the process clean and explicit by hand: macros, documented escalation procedures, a knowledge base that genuinely deflects repeat issues. Then automate the mechanical parts: routing, acknowledgments, data entry. Only then add the AI layer that reads, drafts, and decides. By the time the AI arrives, it is sitting on top of a process that already works, so it amplifies something worth amplifying.

Done in that order, a small team can absorb a lot. A two-person team I ran took on 44% year-over-year ticket growth at a 72% single-reply resolution rate, and the AI was the last piece we added, not the first.

The tell

If a vendor or an internal champion is promising that AI will fix your support operation, that is the signal to slow down. AI is worth adding. It is not a substitute for the work underneath it. The teams that get value from it are the ones that did the boring part first. The teams that get burned bought the multiplier before they had something worth multiplying.

The first piece of that boring part is usually capturing the knowledge that lives in your best people's heads. I wrote about that here, and about how to tell whether the AI is working once it is running here.
