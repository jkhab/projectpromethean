How to Pressure-Test a Support-AI Vendor Before You Buy

Jun 13, 2026 · 5 min read

A demo runs on clean data and happy-path questions. Your support queue is the edges. Here is how to make a vendor show you the parts that do not demo well.

Every support-AI demo looks good, because a demo is run on the cases the vendor chose: clean data, common questions, a happy path. Your actual queue is the opposite. It is the edges, the angry customer, the thing that is not in the knowledge base, the question that needs judgment. The job in an evaluation is to drag the conversation off the happy path and make them show you what happens there. These are the questions that do it.

How do you measure outcome, not activity?

Containment and deflection measure that the AI kept the conversation. They do not measure that the customer got what they came for. Ask how they separate a real resolution from a customer who gave up or had to come back. If the headline number is containment with no recontact rate or satisfaction on contained conversations sitting next to it, you are being shown activity dressed up as outcome. I went deeper on that distinction here.

What is it grounded in, and can we see it?

An AI is only as good as what it knows. Ask exactly what it is grounded in: your real documentation, or a general model guessing about your product. Then ask whether you can see and control that source. A black box you cannot inspect is one you cannot calibrate trust in and cannot fix when it is wrong.

Who referees it, and what happens when it is unsure?

Ask what the AI does when it does not know. Does it guess confidently, or does it hand off, and how does it decide which. Then ask who is in the loop on the answers that do go out. The danger is not the model being wrong sometimes. It is the model being confidently wrong to a customer with nobody positioned to catch it, and that risk is highest on your newest and most junior people, who tend to defer to it.

What does it cost as we scale?

Get past the seat price. Ask what it costs per interaction, what that does as conversations get long, and what the monthly ceiling is. Success raises usage and usage raises cost, so the price of the thing working is part of the deal. I broke down the real cost model here.

What happens at the edges?

Make them run it on your hard cases, not their clean ones. The angry customer, the multi-part question, the issue that is not documented, the one that needs a judgment call. A demo on the easy 80% tells you nothing about the 20% that is most of your actual cost and risk.

The through-line

A vendor confident in the product will show you the failure modes. One who only shows you the demo is hoping you do not ask. Your leverage is highest before you sign, so spend it making them prove the hard parts, not the easy ones.
