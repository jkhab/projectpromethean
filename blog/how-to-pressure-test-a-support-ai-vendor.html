How to Pressure-Test a Support-AI Vendor Before You Buy

Jun 13, 2026 · 5 min read

Every demo looks great, because the vendor picked the knowledge base it runs on. Your queue is the mess. Here are the questions that drag a demo off the happy path, and how to tell a real answer from a polished dodge.

The demo runs on a clean, made-up knowledge base. A few dozen tidy articles, nothing stale, nothing that disagrees. That is not what you own. I built a real knowledge base from scratch, and the live version is contradictions, dead workarounds, and the answer that only lives in one senior agent's head. Your queue is the edges: the angry customer, the thing nobody wrote down, the question that needs judgment. So drag them off the happy path.

Does the customer get what they came for, or did the bot just reply?

A resolved ticket means you took an action. It does not mean the customer got what they came for. Ask how they tell a real fix from a customer who gave up or came back the next day. The dodge you will hear is "we track CSAT on contained chats." That sounds like an answer and it is nearly worthless. Almost nobody answers those surveys, and the ones who do are not the people who gave up. What is hard to game is the same person coming back on the same issue, on any channel. Define what a fix means on your terms and measure it yourself. I went deeper on that here.

Can you see where each answer came from?

Every vendor says yes to "you control your knowledge base." That is the easy yes. The real question is whether you can see, for one specific answer, which doc it pulled from. Then push on the two cases that break things. When two docs disagree, does it tell you or quietly pick one? When it has nothing, does it say it does not know, or does it make something up about your product? The bad failures live in between: a dead article quoted with total confidence, a real quote with one invented sentence stitched in.

What makes it hand off to a human?

"Does it hand off" is the obvious question. The better one is what actually triggers the handoff, and who sets that line. Handing off less makes their numbers look better, so the vendor is paid to set it loose. Then ask who reads the answers that do go out before they reach the customer. The danger is not the bot being wrong sometimes. It is the bot being confidently wrong with nobody there to catch it, and your newest people are the most likely to trust it.

What is it allowed to do, not just say?

Everything above is about a wrong sentence. The newer bots take actions: refunds, cancellations, account changes. A wrong sentence you can walk back. A wrong refund you cannot. Ask exactly what it is allowed to do on its own, what the audit trail is, and whether billing, cancellations, security, and legal are hard-wired to a human no matter how sure the bot is. Then ask if there is a kill switch you can pull yourself, today, without filing a vendor ticket and waiting. I learned this the hard way: a key on one of my own tools got drained, and I rebuilt it with hard caps, alerts, and a kill switch I control.

What does it cost as it scales?

Get past the seat price. The thing nobody warns you about is that success makes your average cost per resolved chat go up. The bot clears the easy, repeat questions first, so what is left skews longer and harder over time. Your longest conversations cost the most, so ask what one of those actually costs, not the average. Caching helps with the repeated part of a long chat, so ask if they do it. And write a hard spending cap into the contract. I shipped a paid AI app with caching and a hard monthly ceiling, so this is one a buyer cannot bluff. I broke down the real cost model here.

How does it do on your real tickets?

Run it on your data, but make the test have teeth. A pile of hard cases you hand over early is just another demo they tune for. Do not send the test set ahead. Pull real, recent tickets it has never seen, including ones your own team got wrong, and run it blind. Then have results reported by your most common ticket types, so you see where it breaks on the questions you actually get most.

What happens six months in?

Every question so far is a snapshot from the day you buy. That is the trap. The model that does well today drifts, your docs fall behind as the product ships, and the vendor can swap the model under you without telling you. So demand a test set you own and can re-run after any change, and a contract trigger if quality drops below a floor. Someone has to keep feeding this thing, and that someone is you, not a vendor whose interest ends at the signature.

The through-line

A vendor proud of the product will show you where it breaks. One who only shows you the demo is hoping you do not ask. Your real leverage is knowing you could stand up the basic version yourself. You do not have to build it. I have, and knowing it can be built is what lets you call the bluff on price and on what is actually hard. That leverage is highest before you sign. The demo is not the game. The renewal is.
