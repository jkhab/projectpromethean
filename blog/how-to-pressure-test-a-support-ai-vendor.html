How to Pressure-Test a Support-AI Vendor Before You Buy

Jun 13, 2026 · 7 min read

A demo runs on a curated corpus and happy-path questions. Your queue is the edges. Here are the questions that make a vendor show you the parts that do not demo well, and the tells that separate a real answer from a polished dodge.

Every support-AI demo looks good, because a demo is run on the corpus the vendor chose: a few dozen knowledge base articles with no stale docs and no two articles that disagree. That is nothing like the corpus you actually own. I architected a real knowledge base from scratch through an Intercom-to-Zendesk migration and a Salesforce transition, and the live thing is contradictions, dead workarounds, and the answer that lives in one senior agent's head. Your queue is the edges: the angry customer, the thing that is not documented, the question that needs judgment. The job in an evaluation is to drag the conversation off the happy path and make them show you what happens there, then to read the difference between a real answer and a polished dodge. These are the questions that do it, and the tells that separate the two.

How do you measure outcome, not activity?

Start by holding them to precise terms. Deflection and containment are not the same metric. Deflection is self-service, measured against attempts, and it counts the rage-quit and the abandoned session as wins. Containment is a bot session that closed without routing to a human. Neither measures that the customer got what they came for. Ask how they separate a real resolution from a customer who gave up or had to come back. The dodge you will hear is "we track CSAT on contained chats." That sounds like a real answer and is nearly worthless: survey response on contained chats is tiny and skews positive, because the customer who quietly gave up is the least likely to fill out a form. The number that is hard to game is recontact on the same issue within a window, across every channel, tied to the same customer, sitting next to an explicit count of abandoned conversations. Define what resolution means on your terms before the POC and instrument it yourself rather than accepting theirs. I ran a two-person team that absorbed 44% year-over-year ticket growth at a 72% single-reply resolution rate by systematizing macros, workflows, and a knowledge base, so I have watched how a number can be technically true and operationally hollow. I went deeper on that distinction here.

What is it grounded in, and can we see it?

A grounded AI is dangerous in proportion to how confidently it fills the gaps in what it does not know, so the question is not whether it is pointed at your docs. Every vendor says yes to "you control your knowledge base." That easy yes conflates owning the source documents with seeing what the system actually retrieved and used. Push to per-answer provenance. For a specific response, can you see which passages were retrieved and which sentences trace to which passage. When two sources conflict, does it tell you or silently pick one. When retrieval comes back empty, does it abstain or fall back to the base model guessing about your product. I loaded full product docs plus years of accumulated nuance into an internal assistant and had to decide what was authoritative in a messy historical corpus, so I know the dangerous failures are not your-docs versus base-model. They live in between: the stale article cited with full confidence, the real citation blended with an invented sentence, the two docs that disagree.

Who referees it, and what happens when it is unsure?

"Confidently wrong" is now a mainstream worry, and "does it hand off" is the obvious question. Make it the mechanism question instead, because a model's stated confidence and its actual correctness are only weakly correlated. Ask what signal actually triggers a handoff: a retrieval score below a threshold, a separate classifier, or nothing. Ask who sets that threshold, and which direction the vendor is incentivized to move it. The abstain dial is usually a retrieval-similarity cutoff, and it sits in direct tension with their headline containment number, so the vendor's incentive is to tune it down. Then ask who is in the loop on the answers that do go out. The danger is not the model being wrong sometimes. It is the model being confidently wrong with nobody positioned to catch it, and that risk is highest on your newest and most junior people, who tend to defer to it.

What is it allowed to do, not just say?

Every question above is about a wrong sentence. Modern agentic support lets the bot take actions: issue a refund, cancel a subscription, change an account setting, reroute a queue. A wrong sentence is recoverable. A wrong write is not. Ask exactly what the AI is permitted to do autonomously, what the audit trail and the rollback path are, and whether your high-stakes categories, billing, cancellation, security, legal, contractual disputes, are hard-wired to a human independent of the model's own confidence. Then ask the question buyers skip because they think of a support AI as a help tool: a customer-facing AI is an externally reachable endpoint, and it can be prompt-injected or driven up in cost by adversarial volume. Ask about per-user rate limiting, and whether there is a kill switch you can pull yourself without filing a vendor ticket and waiting. I learned this the expensive way. A browser-side key on one of my own tools got drained, and I rebuilt it with server-side keys behind a relay, per-IP rate limits, a model whitelist, hard caps with alerts, and an over-the-air kill switch. On another product, crisis-safe escalation is hard-wired to bypass every paywall regardless of what the model decides. Those are blast-radius and forbidden-category controls, and a serious vendor has their own version of every one.

What does it cost as we scale?

Get past the seat price, and do not stop at cost per interaction. Explain the mechanism back to them so you cannot be bluffed. A long multi-turn conversation reprocesses the whole transcript on every turn, so cost grows faster than linearly with length, and prompt caching is the lever that changes that math. Ask whether they cache, what the hit rate is on traffic like yours, and whether the per-interaction price is just their margin on an upstream model that can reprice under you. Then watch the second-order shape, because success makes blended cost per resolution worse. As containment rises, the bot clears the cheap, repeatable tickets first, the ones a good knowledge base already deflects, so the residual it keeps skews longer and harder over time. Model against your real conversation-length distribution, the P95 long conversation, not an average that hides the tail where the budget dies. And treat the ceiling as a mechanism you write into the contract with a defined behavior at the cap, not a number you hope the vendor honors. I shipped a paid AI app with prompt caching that roughly halved a long session's input cost and a hard monthly spend ceiling, so this is the question a buyer cannot bluff: show me your token accounting, and what a P95 long conversation actually costs me. I broke down the real cost model here.

What happens at the edges?

Run it on your data, but do it so the test has teeth. "Test it on hard cases" is not enough, because a pile of hard cases you hand over in advance is just a different demo the vendor tunes for. Do not send the test set ahead of time. Run a blind test drawn live from your queue on tickets the vendor has never seen, and include ones your own team got wrong. Then require results reported per top intent on a volume-weighted slice of your last 90 days, so you see where it breaks on the questions you actually get most, not on an exotic sampler they aced. My two-person team lived in the intent and ticket-category distribution to absorb that 44% growth, and the volume-weighted, by-intent view is the only one that tells you what the thing will cost and risk in production.

What happens six months in?

Every question so far is a snapshot taken at purchase, and that is the trap. The hardest failures only show up after you sign. The model that grounds well today degrades as your product ships features and the knowledge base falls behind, and the vendor can swap the underlying model so your behavior changes underneath you without warning. Ask how drift is detected, who owns knowledge base freshness, how often the model is re-evaluated, whether you are notified before a model change, and what the contractual trigger is if quality falls below a floor. The deliverable to demand is a labeled eval set you own and can re-run yourself after every model or prompt change. I ran an internal assistant as a 24/7 onboarding source for new hires, and it was only ever as current as the docs behind it. Someone has to keep feeding it, and that someone has to be you, not a vendor whose incentive ends at the signature.

The through-line

A vendor confident in the product will show you the failure modes. One who only shows you the demo is hoping you do not ask. The strongest pressure-test is knowing roughly what a thin grounded layer over the model costs to stand up yourself. You do not have to build it. I have built the internal version, a relay with server-side keys, caps, and docs-grounded drafts, and knowing it can be built is what lets you call a vendor's bluff on price and on what is genuinely hard versus what they want you to think is hard. Your leverage is highest before you sign, so spend it converting a demo into protection: an eval set you own, a recontact methodology, retrieval logs, a cost worksheet, all tied to a re-evaluation trigger on a metric floor and an exit ramp. The demo is not the game. The renewal is.
